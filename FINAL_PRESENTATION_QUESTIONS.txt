Final Project Presentation Checklist
At the conclusion of your project, your team will (formally) present at a Senior Capstone “Conference” that is
attended by various stakeholders of the Computer Science Program. Stakeholders include the program's Industry
Advisory Board members, faculty, students, alumni, and other industry representatives.
Use this checklist to ensure your presentation is complete, professional, and aligned with expectations. Your
presentation should be 10-15 minutes and include a short live or recorded demo of your project.
Your presentation should tell a coherent story (problem approach results impact).→ → → Ensure that the
audience can clearly articulate what your project does and why it matters by the end of your
presentation.
Problem and Goals
• Clearly describe the problem or need your project addresses. Be specific! Avoid vague or generic
problems.
• Explain the real-world context (who benefits? what's at stake?).
• Identify key constraints, requirements, or challenges. Include a brief reflection on your proudest successes
and most significant challenges, highlighting a few that you overcame.
Solution Design and Implementation
• Describe your overall solution and system architecture.
• Highlight major technical components and how they work together.
• Justify key design decisions (e.g., why this approach vs. alternatives?).
• Briefly explain your development process or methodology.
• Describe how you tested or evaluated your system.
• Acknowledge any known limitations or areas for future work.
• Include any relevant design or fabrication aspects (e.g., use of the Makerspace). If your project includes a
significant hardware component, discuss it along with the software component.
• Clearly communicate the results or outcomes of your project (what worked, how well did it perform, etc).

Communication and Professionalism
• Dress in professional or business casual attire appropriate for a public presentation. Arrive early,
prepared, and focused.
• Maintain professional demeanor. Be respectful, engaged, and attentive throughout the presentation.
• At the beginning of your presentation, introduce yourselves and the project (title/description).
• Present in a clear, well-organized, and professional manner. Speak audibly and at a reasonable pace.
Minimize filler words (“um,” “like”) and avoid reading directly from slides.
• Use visuals/slides that are clean, readable, and purposeful.
• Respond to questions thoughtfully and respectfully.
• Present for a mixed audience (both technical and non-technical stakeholders).
Ethics, Legal, and Societal Considerations
• Identify any ethical, legal, or security-related concerns in your project.
• Address user privacy, accessibility, or data security, if relevant.
• Consider your project's impact on individuals, organizations, or society.
Team Collaboration
• Ensure each team member presents and explains part of the work.
• Explain how your team divided tasks and responsibilities.
• Reflect briefly on teamwork successes or challenges.
Technical Depth
• Demonstrate appropriate technical knowledge during explanations.
• Use accurate terminology and show understanding of CS concepts.
• Reference relevant theories, data structures, algorithms, or tools (basically, bring in things you learned
throughout the CS curriculum).
Demo and Visuals
• Include a title slide with project name and team members.

• Avoid using a lot of text on slides. Images are better. Practice what you intend to say, and use images as a
trigger for that.
• Include a live or recorded demonstration of your system. Ensure the demo highlights core features or user
experience.
• Narrate the demo. Explain what the audience is seeing and why it matters.
• Focus on the most important aspects of your project. Don't try to show everything.
• Test your demo setup in advance to avoid technical issues.
• Back up your demo with screenshots or a contingency plan just in case.
• Use an emulator to showcase mobile apps and/or provide a QR code that the audience can use to
download your app.
Timing and Transitions
• Practice to ensure your presentation fits in 10-15 minutes. This is important! I will stop your
presentation (“pull the plug”) and transition to another group after 15 minutes (which includes some time
for Q&A).
• Use smooth transitions between speakers.
Optional: Supporting Materials
• Provide a QR code or link to your GitHub repo or documentation.
• Acknowledge any external tools, APIs, or libraries used. Include limitations (e.g., query limits, etc). Include
AI tools as well.
• Make sure that your GitHub repo is updated, organized (e.g., don't put everything in the top-level folder),
etc. Including all phase documents (requirements, design, sprints, etc) and presentations is strongly
recommended.
• Ensure that your Trello boards are also updated and well-organized.
• Anticipate likely questions and prepare responses in advance.
